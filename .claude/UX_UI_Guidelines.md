Somenta - UX/UI & Interaction Guidelines

⚠️ AI Agent Directive: Role & Scope
Your objective is to elevate the provided design by introducing purposeful motion, refined spacing, and enhanced aesthetics. However, you must execute these improvements within strict psychological guardrails to protect the user's cognitive load and emotional safety. You are permitted to enhance the layout and interactivity, but you must strictly adhere to the following UX laws.

1. Visual Hierarchy & Contrast (The 60-30-10 Rule & Von Restorff Effect)
•	CRITICAL INSTRUCTION - Preserve the Color Palette: You must strictly use the exact color palette provided in the design assets. Do not introduce new colors, alter the existing hex codes, or change the background colors. Your goal is to maintain the exact calming, premium emotional resonance of the original design.
•	The 60-30-10 Rule: The current design largely implements a 60-30-10 color distribution (60% dominant/calming background, 30% secondary/structural, 10% accent). As you enhance the layout, motion, and spacing, you must maintain this exact balance.
•	The Restraint Rule (Von Restorff Effect): When multiple similar objects are present, the one that differs from the rest is most likely to be remembered. You must use severe restraint when placing emphasis on visual elements. The 10% accent color must be reserved almost exclusively for the primary CTA ("Find your path"). Do not dilute this focal point by adding unnecessary color accents elsewhere.
•	Color Contrast: Ensure the existing color contrast is maintained and that any layout changes do not place light text on light backgrounds or vice versa, ensuring a minimum contrast ratio of 4.5:1 between text and its background.

2. Layout, Spacing, & Cognitive Load (Miller’s Law & Hick’s Law)
You may refine the layout to make it more aesthetically pleasing and digestible, but you must not increase the user's cognitive load.
•	Chunking: Organize content into smaller, visually distinct clusters to help users process and understand the information easily. Use negative space, scale, and subtle dividers to group related content and prevent overwhelming "walls of text".
•	Avoid Oversimplification: While streamlining the interface, do not simplify it to the point of abstraction. Hiding critical information or relying solely on vague icons without text labels actually increases cognitive load. Ensure all actions and next steps remain crystal clear.
•	The Aesthetic-Usability Effect: Strive for an aesthetically pleasing design, as beautiful design creates a positive emotional response that leads users to perceive the site as more usable and trustworthy.

3. Motion, Animation, & Performance (Doherty Threshold)
You are encouraged to add motion to make the experience feel premium and responsive, but it must be meticulously calibrated for accessibility.
•	Purposeful Micro-Interactions: Focus on micro-interactions that have a clear purpose to provide feedback, guide the user, and enhance usability, rather than just serving as decoration. These small, subtle animations—such as a gentle, satisfying response when hovering over the "Find your path" button or selecting an assessment answer—make the interface feel more intuitive, responsive, and deeply human.
•	Motion Narrative: Avoid flashy, decorative hover effects and instead use intentional, thoughtful movement to tell a story and gently guide the user through the site. Employ subtle scroll-triggered animations or elegant page transitions that create a seamless, cinematic flow. This ensures the user feels like an active participant in an immersive, calming experience.
•	Performance-First Creativity: A beautiful website is useless if it is slow, clunky, and frustrating to use. Productivity and engagement soar when a system responds in under 400 milliseconds. Use smooth animations (such as skeleton screens or subtle transitions) to visually engage users while any loading or processing happens in the background, ensuring that fast performance is treated as a core part of the design process.
•	Accessibility Guardrails for Motion: You must carefully consider users with motion sensitivity or vestibular disorders. Do not distract the user with flashy UI or quirky, erratic, or overly fast animations. Motion should be smooth, purposeful, and used only to gently guide attention.

4. Interaction & Accessibility (Fitts’s Law)
As you refine the layout and add interactive elements, ensure the mechanics of the site remain effortless to use across all devices.
•	Touch Targets & Spacing: The time to acquire a target is a function of the distance to and size of the target. Ensure all interactive elements (buttons, form fields, quiz selections) are large enough for users to accurately select them, and provide ample spacing between them to prevent accidental clicks.
•	Color Contrast: Do not exclude users with a color vision deficiency or low vision. Ensure a minimum color contrast ratio of 4.5:1 between text and its background. Never rely exclusively on color to communicate contrast or interactive states; utilize additional cues like subtle strokes or shapes.
